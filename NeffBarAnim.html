At first glance, it may seem obvious why human populations grow: The number of births per year exceeds the number of deaths. To some extent this is true, if we ignore immigration (the movement of people into a population) and emigration (the movement of people out of a population). But the basic population growth equation (births minus deaths) is influenced by a complex set of factors, including life expectancy, gender ratios (ratio of males to females), birth and death rates, and ages of people in the population. All of these statistics change through time and can greatly affect the rate of population growth.

One of the most important predictors of how quickly (or slowly) a population will grow in the future is the relative number of younger and older people in the population. We can think about this in a simple way by imagining one population of 1,000 60-year-olds and another one made up of 1,000 15-year-olds. Which of these two populations would grow faster in the future? The answer is the 15-year-olds because they are able to have their own children for the next 25 or 30 years whereas most 60-year-olds are reaching the end of their reproductive lives. All populations have an age structure, or relative number of people in each age class. If we ignore all other factors, populations with more people in younger age groups will grow faster in the future than populations with a larger amount of people in older age groups.

An age structure can also be used to calculate the dependency ratio, the ratio of nonworking people (children under 15 and adults over 65) to working people (ages 15-65). A high dependency ratio can put a strain on a country's economy because nonworking people including children and the elderly rely more heavily on social services compared to working adults.

Many countries in sub-Saharan Africa have high dependency ratios because of large numbers of children as the result of high birth rates and adult mortality due, in large part, to the HIV/AIDS epidemic described later in this chapter. In some of these countries, large numbers of orphaned children can create humanitarian crises and can become targets for militias and armies willing to use child soldiers.

In the United States, Japan, and some European countries, the dependency ratios are increasing for a different reason: growing numbers of people over the age of 65. In these countries, a growing population creates financial stress as elderly people need more access to health care and other social services.
